It's Official: Scientists Confirmed What Is Inside Our Moon

A detailed investigation has found the Moon's inner core is a solid ball with a density similar to iron, settling a long-running scientific debate. This confirmation provides a clearer understanding of the Moon's history.

BY Michelle Starr

A thorough investigation has confirmed the Moon‚Äôs inner core is a solid ball with a density similar to that of iron. This major finding resolves a long-standing debate about whether the lunar core was solid or molten, advancing our understanding of the Moon‚Äôs, and the solar system's, history.

The research, led by astronomer Arthur Briaud of the French National Centre for Scientific Research, used data from several space missions and laser ranging experiments to build a comprehensive model of the lunar interior. The models that best fit the observational data indicate that the Moon's core is very similar to Earth's, possessing a fluid outer layer and a solid inner core.

Core Confirmation
Data modelling confirms a solid inner core and fluid outer core.

This discovery has significant implications for lunar geology. The models support the theory of an active mantle overturn deep within the Moon, where denser materials sank towards the core while less dense materials rose. This process could help explain the presence of iron-rich materials in volcanic rocks found on the lunar surface, providing a mechanism for their transport from the deep interior.

Understanding the lunar core‚Äôs composition is also vital for piecing together the history of the Moon‚Äôs magnetic field. It is known that the Moon once had a powerful magnetic field that has since disappeared. A solid inner core and fluid outer core are essential components for a planetary dynamo, the mechanism that generates such fields.

"This, researchers hope, will help settle a long debate about whether the Moon's inner heart is solid or molten."

The new findings align with and confirm earlier research from 2011, which used state-of-the-art seismological techniques on Apollo-era data to suggest the presence of a solid inner core. However, the resolution of that older data was too low to make a definitive determination, leaving room for debate until now.

A Faded Magnetism
The core's structure is key to understanding the loss of the Moon‚Äôs magnetic field.

By defining the structure of the lunar core more precisely, scientists can now refine their models of how the Moon‚Äôs magnetic dynamo functioned and why it eventually ceased billions of years ago. This knowledge contributes not just to the Moon‚Äôs story but to the broader understanding of how planetary bodies in our Solar System form and evolve over time.

Future missions to the Moon, particularly those deploying more sensitive seismic instruments, will aim to further validate these findings. A clearer picture of the lunar interior will provide invaluable data for understanding the early conditions of our solar system and the formation of rocky planets like Earth.

The Core Debate

For decades, scientists debated whether the Moon‚Äôs heart was a solid sphere or entirely molten. Early seismic data collected during the Apollo missions was not detailed enough to provide a clear answer, as models for both a solid and a fully fluid core could fit the limited data.

Resolving this question is crucial for understanding the Moon's thermal history and the mechanisms that once powered its now-defunct magnetic field. This recent confirmation of a solid inner core provides a definitive answer and a stronger foundation for lunar science.
