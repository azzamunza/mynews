Space radiation can produce some organic molecules on icy moons

By Javier BarbuzanoOct 26, 20251 min read

New laboratory research indicates that some organic molecules found in the plumes of Saturn's moon Enceladus could be the result of natural radiation, complicating the search for life.

This discovery may require scientists to reassess the astrobiological significance of these compounds, as they may not originate from the moon’s subsurface ocean as previously hoped. As future missions prepare to visit ocean worlds like Enceladus and Europa, researchers will need to develop methods to distinguish between organics created by radiation and those that might genuinely indicate a habitable environment.

Key Takeaways

  • Space radiation can form organic molecules on icy moons.
  • This discovery complicates the search for life, as these molecules may not be from biological sources.
  • Future space missions will need to differentiate between radiation-formed and potentially life-indicating organics.
