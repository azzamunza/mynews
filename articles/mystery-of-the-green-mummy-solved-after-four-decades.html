Mystery of the Green Mummy Solved After Four Decades

Scientific analysis has finally revealed why the mummified remains of an Italian teenager took on a unique emerald hue, solving a long-standing archaeological puzzle.

BY Ancient Origins

After four decades of speculation, new analysis of a teenager's mummified remains has revealed the chemical secrets behind its unique green colouration. Discovered in Bologna, Italy, in 1987, the body puzzled scientists, but recent studies show a post-mortem chemical reaction with its copper coffin is responsible for the startling preservation and colour.

The adolescent, dated to have died between 1617 and 1814, was buried in a copper-alloy casket which cracked over time. Fluids from the body reacted with the metal, creating copper corrosion products that infiltrated the remains. This process, known as ionic substitution, systematically replaced calcium in the bones with copper ions, tinting the tissues green and aiding in their preservation.

This case represents the most complete example of copper-induced mummification ever recorded, offering significant insights into how burial materials can interact with human remains. While copper's antimicrobial properties are known to inhibit decay, this discovery demonstrates a deeper level of tissue and bone penetration.

A Chemical Transformation
The remains were discovered in a copper-alloy coffin, which led to a unique chemical reaction causing the green colouration.

Detailed analysis revealed that copper levels in some bones were several hundred times higher than average. The preservation was also aided by the cool, low-oxygen environment of the basement where the mummy was found. Interestingly, the left leg retained a more natural colour, likely because its position prevented direct contact with the corroding copper.

"This completely changes our point of view on the role of heavy metals, as their effects on preservation are more complex than we might expect."

Researchers used advanced techniques like Raman spectroscopy and Scanning Electron Microscopy to understand the chemical process. Their findings help archaeologists better interpret burial practices and the complex post-mortem changes that can occur in ancient remains. The cause of the teenager's death remains unknown, as no signs of trauma or disease were found.

The study highlights a fascinating and rare preservation phenomenon. It stands in contrast to more intentional forms of mummification, such as those in ancient Egypt, by showcasing a unique, accidental process driven by the burial environment and materials.

A Unique Case Study
The Bologna mummy provides an unprecedented look at copper-induced preservation.

This discovery provides a valuable case study for understanding taphonomy—the study of how organisms decay and become fossilised. The Green Mummy of Bologna will continue to offer insights into the unexpected ways our history can be preserved and revealed through scientific investigation.

Key Context

The Bologna mummy is a case of accidental, natural mummification heavily influenced by artificial materials. Unlike the embalming techniques of ancient Egypt, this individual's preservation was the result of a specific set of environmental conditions and a chemical reaction with its copper coffin.

This phenomenon, where copper ions replace calcium in bones, is extremely rare and has never been documented to this complete extent before. The discovery provides crucial data for archaeologists studying burial customs of the European elite during this period.

A New Chapter in Preservation Science
The Green Mummy of Bologna has rewritten our understanding of how metals in burial sites can lead to extraordinary forms of preservation.
