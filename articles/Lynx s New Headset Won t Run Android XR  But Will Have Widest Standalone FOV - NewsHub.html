Lynx’s New Headset Won’t Run Android XR, But Will Have Widest Standalone FOV

A surprising pivot from the French startup sees it part ways with Google’s XR platform, while promising a major leap in immersive technology.

BY David Heaney

French startup Lynx has revealed its upcoming headset will not run Google‚Äôs Android XR operating system due to a terminated agreement. Instead, the device will feature a record-breaking 170-degree field of view, the widest of any standalone headset, aiming to significantly boost user immersion in virtual reality.

The decision to forgo Android XR was described by Lynx as a "surprising turn of events" after Google reportedly terminated their agreement. As a result, the new headset will operate on a forked version of Android, complete with its own services and app store. This approach is similar to how ByteDance manages its Pico headsets within China.

A New Horizon
The new headset promises a 170-degree field of view, a substantial increase over current models.

A Leap in Immersion The standout feature is the headset’s expansive diagonal field of view, achieved through new patent-pending lenses. At 170 degrees, it comes remarkably close to the human visual field of around 200 degrees and far surpasses the 100-110 degree standard seen in most current consumer VR headsets.

This development follows the company’s experience with its first product, the Lynx-R1, a mixed reality device that also aimed for a wider-than-average FOV but encountered significant production delays and price adjustments after its initial Kickstarter campaign.

"We remain open to having Android XR running on our devices in the future, should Google change its stance."

Despite the software pivot, Lynx founder Stan Larroque remains hopeful for future collaboration with Google. The company plans a formal announcement for the new headset in early 2026, with pre-orders expected to open simultaneously. The final price has not yet been determined.

Forging Its Own Path
Lynx will develop a custom Android-based OS and app store for its new device.

The path for Lynx has been challenging, with the founder previously describing the Lynx-R1's production as an "absolute mess". However, these lessons in supply chain management have informed the strategy for the new device, which will not be crowdfunded.

With a focus on groundbreaking optics and a newly independent software ecosystem, Lynx is positioning its next-generation headset to compete on immersion and performance, even without the backing of Google's official XR platform.

The new device builds on lessons learned from the Lynx-R1.

The Lynx-R1 Journey

Lynx’s first headset, the Lynx-R1, was announced in 2020 and generated significant interest through a successful Kickstarter campaign. It was one of the first consumer-focused standalone headsets to promise colour passthrough for mixed reality.

However, the project faced numerous delays and a price increase, eventually being surpassed to market by competitors like the Meta Quest Pro. These challenges provided the company with valuable, albeit difficult, experience in hardware production and supply chain logistics.
