Northern lights may be visible in 10 US states Oct. 29

A minor G1 geomagnetic storm, caused by solar winds from a coronal hole, is set to make the aurora visible across parts of the northern United States.

BY Daisy Dobrijevic

The northern lights are expected to be visible across 10 U.S. states on October 29th, a result of a minor (G1) geomagnetic storm. This celestial display is caused by a stream of fast solar wind from a large coronal hole hitting Earth’s magnetic field, according to NOAA’s Space Weather Prediction Center.

Forecasters at the SWPC have predicted these geomagnetic storm conditions to persist from October 29th through to October 30th. The storm is a direct consequence of a high-speed stream of solar wind emanating from a significant, Earth-facing coronal hole.

According to the latest aurora forecast map from NOAA, residents in ten specific U.S. states have the potential to witness the aurora. These states include Alaska, North Dakota, Montana, Minnesota, Wisconsin, Idaho, Washington, South Dakota, Michigan, and Maine.

The aurora borealis is the stunning result of solar particles interacting with Earth's magnetic field.

The ongoing solar activity provides a rare opportunity for skywatchers in the contiguous United States to experience a phenomenon usually reserved for higher latitudes. The G1 storm classification is considered minor but is sufficient to push the auroral oval southwards.

"A stream of fast solar wind from a large Earth-facing coronal hole continues to buffet Earth's magnetic field, creating a dazzling display."

For the best viewing experience, it is recommended to find a location away from city lights, with a clear view of the northern horizon. The peak activity is often unpredictable but typically occurs late at night.

This event highlights the dynamic connection between the Sun and Earth, where solar phenomena can have beautiful and observable effects on our planet.

Geomagnetic activity makes the northern lights visible further south than usual.

Viewers are encouraged to check the latest updates from NOAA's Space Weather Prediction Center for real-time forecasts, as conditions can change rapidly.

What is a Coronal Hole?

A coronal hole is a region on the Sun’s surface where the magnetic field opens up, allowing high-speed solar wind to stream out into space. When this wind reaches Earth, it interacts with our planet’s magnetosphere, triggering geomagnetic storms that can produce vibrant auroras.

These events are a normal part of the Sun's activity cycle and are monitored by agencies like NOAA.
