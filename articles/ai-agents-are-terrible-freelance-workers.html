AI Agents Are Terrible Freelance Workers

Even the best artificial intelligence agents are fairly hopeless at online freelance work, according to an experiment that challenges the idea of AI replacing office workers.

BY Will Knight

A recent experiment has revealed that even the most advanced artificial intelligence agents are largely ineffective at performing online freelance work, challenging the narrative that AI will soon replace office workers en masse. This finding comes from a new benchmark designed to test AI's real-world economic capabilities.

The benchmark, known as The Remote Labor Index, was developed by researchers at data annotation company Scale AI in collaboration with the Center for AI Safety (CAIS), a nonprofit organisation. It is designed to measure the ability of frontier AI models to automate economically valuable work by assigning them a variety of simulated freelance tasks.

The study involved giving several leading AI agents a range of these tasks to complete. The results were stark: the top-performing AI model could only complete less than 3 percent of the assigned work, indicating a significant gap between current AI capabilities and the requirements of autonomous professional work.

AI's Economic Value
Researchers are developing new benchmarks to test the practical ability of AI agents in the modern workforce.

Financially, the AI agents fell dramatically short. Out of a potential earning pool of $143,991 available across all tasks, the most capable AI agent managed to earn only $1,810. This outcome highlights the complexities and nuances of freelance work that AI is not yet equipped to handle, such as client communication, subjective feedback, and creative problem-solving.

"Even the best could perform less than 3 percent of the work, earning $1,810 out of a possible $143,991."

These findings suggest that while AI is a powerful tool for specific, well-defined tasks, its ability to operate as an autonomous freelance worker is still in its infancy. The idea of AI agents replacing human professionals on a large scale remains a distant prospect, according to this research.

The study underscores the importance of focusing on AI as a tool to augment human capabilities rather than replace them entirely. The path to fully autonomous AI in the workplace requires significant advancements in reasoning, adaptability, and understanding of complex human instructions.

Key Context

The Remote Labor Index is a new benchmark created by Scale AI and the Center for AI Safety (CAIS). Its primary purpose is to quantitatively measure how well today's most advanced AI models can perform valuable economic tasks that are commonly found on freelance platforms.

Unlike other benchmarks that test isolated skills, this index evaluates AI agents on their ability to handle entire workflows, from understanding instructions to delivering a final product, providing a more holistic view of their job-readiness.
