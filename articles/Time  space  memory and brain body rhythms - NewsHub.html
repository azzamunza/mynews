Time, space, memory and brain‚Äìbody rhythms

A new perspective in neuroscience suggests our perception of time and space is not directly sensed, but is instead an emergent property of the complex interplay between our brain and body rhythms.

BY Gy√∂rgy Buzs√°ki

The intuitive concepts of space and time are fundamentally challenged within modern neuroscience. Contrary to classical physics, our brains do not possess sensors to perceive space or time directly. Instead, neuronal activity is better understood as a sequence of events, from which our spatial and temporal awareness is constructed.

For decades, investigators of navigation and memory have linked neuronal activity to measurements of position, distance, and duration. While correlations between brain activity and these physical parameters exist, they may not represent a direct neural encoding of space or time. This approach treats these concepts as separate coordinates, unlike the unified spacetime of modern physics.

Internal Rhythms
Neuronal activity operates as a succession of events, not a direct measurement of external coordinates.

This revised view proposes that experienced time is linked to a hierarchy of brain-body rhythms. These internal clocks provide a range of reference scales that form our subjective feeling of time's passage. The brain does not simply record external reality; it generates inferential models to explain it, with space and time being core components of these mental constructs.

This perspective helps resolve the tension between the linear, unidirectional flow of physical time and the flexible, variable nature of our experienced time. It shifts the focus from finding where the brain 'stores' time to understanding how it 'creates' time through its intrinsic operations.

"Neither instruments nor brains sense space or time. Neuronal activity can be described as a succession of events without resorting to the concepts of space or time."

The hippocampus, long associated with spatial mapping through 'place cells', is central to this discussion. The discovery of 'time cells' further supports the idea that this brain region is crucial for coding episodic memories‚Äîthe what, where, and when of our personal experiences. However, the new framework suggests these cells are part of a broader mechanism for sequencing events, rather than mapping onto an absolute grid of space and time.

Memory Construction
Episodic memories are defined by what happened, where it happened, and when.

Ultimately, neuroscience is moving away from the Newtonian view of the brain as a device that perceives pre-existing, independent categories of space and time. Instead, it is embracing a model where these fundamental concepts are constructed internally.

The implications are profound, suggesting that our entire experience of reality is a model built upon the foundation of rhythmic biological processes. Changes in our body and brain circuits, tied to these rhythms, may be the ultimate source of our subjective experience of the world and our place within it.

A Shift in Perspective

For a long time, neuroscience operated on the assumption that space and time were external realities that the brain needed to perceive and represent accurately. This "outside-in" approach sought to find the specific neurons or brain regions that acted as the internal equivalent of a clock or a GPS.

The current view, articulated by researchers like Gy√∂rgy Buzs√°ki, flips this idea. It suggests an "inside-out" model where the brain's own internal, self-organised sequential activity is primary. Our concepts of space and time are then constructed upon this foundation to make sense of our interactions with the world.
