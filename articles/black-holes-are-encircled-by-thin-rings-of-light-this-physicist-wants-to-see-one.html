Black holes are encircled by thin rings of light. This physicist wants to see one

A mysterious ring of light is thought to surround every black hole, and Alex Lupsasca wants to glimpse one for the first time. The tendril-thin halo, called a photon ring, has the power to reveal secrets from the black hole's edge.

BY Emily Conover

A mysterious, tendril-thin halo of light is thought to encircle every black hole, and physicist Alex Lupsasca is on a mission to see one for the first time. This photon ring, comprised of light particles orbiting the black hole's edge, could unlock secrets about these enigmatic cosmic objects and confirm fundamental theories of physics.

Black holes represent regions where spacetime is so intensely warped that nothing, not even light, can escape. However, physicists theorise that photons can graze this point of no return, orbiting one or more times before escaping. This trapped and released light forms the photon ring, a feature that Lupsasca believes is the definitive proof of a black hole's existence.

Lupsasca, a theoretical physicist at Vanderbilt University, serves as the project scientist for the Black Hole Explorer. His research focuses on the properties of the photon ring that the team aims to detect. The mission's success would provide an unprecedented test of physicists' theories about the universe's most extreme objects.

First Image of a Black Hole
The Event Horizon Telescope collaboration captured the first direct visual evidence of a supermassive black hole and its shadow in the Messier 87 galaxy.

Observing the unseeable is a monumental challenge. Spotting the photon ring requires extraordinary precision and technology. Confirming its existence would not only validate current models of black holes but also rule out alternative, more exotic explanations for these cosmic phenomena. Lupsasca's work is crucial in defining what to look for.

"Seeing this photon ring and confirming that it's really produced by orbiting photons would be ironclad, definitive proof that the object that we're looking at really is a black hole."

The quest to observe the photon ring is a significant step in astrophysics. The first image of a black hole, M87*, provided a groundbreaking look at its shadow, but the photon ring itself remained unresolved. Future projects, like the Black Hole Explorer, aim to achieve the necessary resolution to finally see this faint halo of light.

Understanding the photon ring's structure could reveal key properties of the black hole, such as its mass and spin, with incredible accuracy. This would offer a new window into the fundamental physics of gravity and the behaviour of matter in the most extreme environments known to science.

M87 Galaxy's Core
The supermassive black hole at the centre of the Messier 87 galaxy is one of the primary targets for observation.

Lupsasca's dedication highlights a pivotal moment in physics, where theoretical predictions are becoming testable through technological advancement. The successful detection of a photon ring would mark a historic achievement in our quest to understand the universe's deepest mysteries.
